APPENDIX E: INTERVIEW MINDSET & COMMUNICATION TIPS
==================================================================

TECHNICAL COMMUNICATION FRAMEWORK
------------------------------------

When answering ANY technical question, use this structure:
1. WHAT it is (1 sentence definition)
2. WHY it exists (problem it solves)
3. HOW it works (mechanism)
4. WHEN to use it (vs alternatives)
5. Real-world example from your experience

Example — "What is caching?":
WHAT: Caching stores computed or fetched results to serve future requests faster.
WHY: Database/computation is expensive; serving from memory is ~100x faster.
HOW: On read, check cache first. On miss, compute and store with TTL.
WHEN: Read-heavy data that changes infrequently. Not for real-time critical data.
EXAMPLE: In my last project, I cached the product catalog in Redis with 15-min TTL —
         API response time dropped from 400ms to 25ms.

CODING INTERVIEW COMMUNICATION:
1. Repeat the problem to confirm understanding.
2. Clarify constraints: "Can input be empty? Negative numbers? Duplicates?"
3. Discuss approach before coding: "I'm thinking BFS here because..."
4. Think aloud while coding — interviewers want to hear reasoning.
5. Test with example before claiming done.
6. Discuss complexity: O(n) time, O(n) space.
7. Suggest improvements: "This could be optimized to O(log n) with..."

SYSTEM DESIGN COMMUNICATION:
Start broad, go deep on request. Say: "I'll start with the high-level design and we can deep-dive wherever you're most interested." This shows maturity — you know you can't cover everything equally.

HANDLING UNKNOWN QUESTIONS:
Don't fake it. Say: "I haven't worked with X directly, but from what I know about Y (related thing), I'd expect it to work like Z. Can you confirm if that's the right direction?" Shows: intellectual honesty + reasoning skills.

FOR THE DRIVER GAPS:
"I haven't written production drivers yet, but I've been actively studying this area — I've built a simple character device module in Linux and worked through the KMDF echo sample in Windows. I understand the concepts at a foundational level and I'm confident I can be productive with mentorship from your team."

BEHAVIORAL QUESTION FRAMEWORK (STAR):
Situation: context (brief)
Task: what you were responsible for
Action: what YOU specifically did (not "we")
Result: quantified outcome

NUMBERS MATTER IN RESULTS:
Not: "The app got faster" → "API latency dropped from 1.2s to 180ms for P95"
Not: "I fixed a bug" → "Fixed a race condition causing 0.3% of orders to double-charge"
Not: "I improved tests" → "Increased test coverage from 23% to 71% over 3 months"

SALARY NEGOTIATION MINDSET:
Research market rate first (levels.fyi, Glassdoor, LinkedIn Salary, local benchmarks).
For a role requiring both software expertise AND driver learning: you bring the software skills immediately, driver gap is filled by training investment on their side. Frame accordingly.
Let them make first offer when possible. Counter with data, not just desire.
